Deploying computer vision on embedded platforms — cameras, edge devices, microcontrollers — introduces constraints that are largely absent from server-side inference. Power budgets, memory limits, latency requirements, and the absence of a GPU fundamentally shape which architectures are viable.

This post outlines the main design challenges and strategies for efficient embedded vision.

## The embedded constraint space

Unlike cloud inference, embedded vision must operate within tight and often non-negotiable limits:

* **Memory**: SRAM is typically in the range of kilobytes to a few megabytes. Even small models may not fit without quantization or architectural changes.
* **Compute**: Integer arithmetic is often preferred over floating point. SIMD instructions may be available, but vary widely across platforms.
* **Power**: Battery-powered devices may allow only milliwatts for vision processing, making energy-per-inference a primary metric.
* **Latency**: Real-time applications require inference to complete within a frame budget — often 33 ms for 30 fps video.

<KeyPoint title="Design principle">
On embedded platforms, the dominant cost is often memory bandwidth, not arithmetic. Architectures that minimize data movement — not just FLOP count — tend to perform best in practice.
</KeyPoint>

## Architectural strategies

Several strategies have emerged to address embedded constraints:

**Depthwise separable convolutions** decompose standard convolutions into a spatial filtering step and a pointwise mixing step. This reduces computation by a factor of 8–9× at a modest accuracy cost.

**Quantization** reduces weight and activation precision, typically from 32-bit float to 8-bit integer. Post-training quantization requires no additional training and often achieves near-baseline accuracy on classification tasks.

**Pruning** removes redundant weights or entire filters from a trained network. Structured pruning (removing entire channels) is preferred on hardware that cannot exploit irregular sparsity.

**Knowledge distillation** transfers the behaviour of a large model into a smaller one, allowing the compact model to benefit from the capacity of its teacher during training.

## Deployment toolchains

Efficient deployment also depends on the software stack. Frameworks such as TensorFlow Lite, ONNX Runtime, and TVM provide tools for model conversion, optimization, and hardware-specific code generation.

Selecting the right toolchain for a given target platform often has as much impact on performance as the architectural choices themselves.

## Remaining challenges

Efficient embedded vision remains an active research area. Key open problems include:

* Automated neural architecture search for resource-constrained targets.
* Reliable accuracy-efficiency trade-off evaluation across heterogeneous hardware.
* Handling dynamic scenes and variable illumination with minimal compute.
