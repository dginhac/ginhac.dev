Most computer vision systems are trained on standard, single-exposure images. Yet real-world scenes exhibit a dynamic range far exceeding what any single exposure can capture.

The LUMEN project investigates a different starting point: what if we treated multiple exposures not as inputs to be merged, but as complementary views of the same underlying scene luminance?

## Motivation

Camera sensors operate within a limited dynamic range. When capturing a scene, any given exposure setting will either clip bright regions or lose detail in shadows. A sequence of images taken at different exposure times, however, collectively spans the full luminance range of the scene.

This multi-exposure structure provides a natural learning signal: if two patches from different exposures show the same physical surface, their representations should agree — regardless of the local exposure level.

<KeyPoint title="Central hypothesis">
Exposure variation is a nuisance for recognition, but a signal for representation learning. Training a model to be invariant to exposure changes encourages it to encode the underlying physical luminance, rather than the sensor-specific rendering.
</KeyPoint>

## Approach

LUMEN frames multi-exposure sequences as a self-supervised learning problem. Given two crops from different exposures of the same scene, the model is trained to produce representations that are close in embedding space.

Key design choices:

* **Exposure-aware augmentation** — the training pipeline explicitly includes radiometric transformations to simulate various exposure levels.
* **Contrastive objective** — pairs of crops from different exposures are treated as positive pairs; crops from different scenes are negatives.
* **Architecture** — a standard vision encoder (ViT or ResNet) is used without modification; the exposure invariance emerges from the training procedure.

## Preliminary results

Early experiments show that LUMEN representations transfer well to downstream tasks involving challenging lighting conditions, including low-light detection and overexposed scene segmentation.

They also show improved consistency compared to representations trained on single-exposure data, measured by the agreement between features extracted from different exposures of the same scene.

## Open questions

Several questions remain active:

* What is the right temporal scope for multi-exposure training data — burst sequences, bracketed captures, or synthetic generation?
* How does exposure invariance interact with other nuisance factors such as motion blur and noise?
* Can LUMEN representations serve as a foundation for HDR-specific downstream tasks?
