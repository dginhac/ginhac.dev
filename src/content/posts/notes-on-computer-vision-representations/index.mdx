Visual representations sit at the core of every computer vision system. Before any model can detect, segment, or classify, it must first encode the visual world into a form it can reason about.

This question — how to represent images — has driven decades of research and continues to shape the field today.

## From hand-crafted to learned features

Early computer vision relied on carefully designed features: edge detectors, gradient histograms, local descriptors. These methods encoded domain knowledge directly into the representation, with explicit assumptions about what visual structure matters.

The shift toward learned representations changed this fundamentally. Instead of prescribing what to look for, we now let models discover which features are predictive for a given task or dataset.

<KeyPoint title="Key tension">
Hand-crafted features are interpretable and data-efficient, but rigid. Learned features are flexible and powerful, but can overfit to task-specific biases and require large datasets to generalize.
</KeyPoint>

## Self-supervised and task-agnostic representations

A more recent direction moves away from task-specific supervision altogether. Self-supervised methods learn representations without explicit labels, using the structure of the data itself as a learning signal.

Contrastive methods, masked autoencoders, and joint embedding architectures have each proposed different ways to define what "similar" means for visual data. The goal is always the same: a representation that captures useful structure, without being anchored to a single downstream task.

This connects to the broader aspiration of foundation models in vision — representations general enough to be repurposed across many tasks with minimal adaptation.

## What makes a good representation?

Several properties are commonly sought:

* **Invariance** to nuisance factors (lighting, viewpoint, noise)
* **Equivariance** to meaningful transformations (rotation, scale)
* **Compactness** — a small code that captures most of the relevant structure
* **Transferability** — performance on unseen tasks with little or no fine-tuning

These properties are often in tension, and the right balance depends on the application.
